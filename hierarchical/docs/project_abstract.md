Hierarchical Bayesian Learning as a Mechanism for Perceptual Switching
Human perceptual estimates are often modeled as a Bayesian system to reflect the integration of noisy sensory estimates (likelihood) with expected prior(s). However, this results in a posterior distribution that does not reflect the bimodality observed in motion-direction estimation tasks, with peaks near both the prior and the true direction. This motivated previous research to use a Switching observer that selects between one source (prior or likelihood) per trial instead. Although it successfully captured bimodal response distributions, the switching mechanism is specified a priori and assumes a fixed competition between prior and sensory evidence, without modeling how confidence in the prior is learned from experience. We therefore ask, can a Bayesian observer that learns how much to trust its prior, trial by trial, reproduce the bimodal perceptual estimates previously attributed to an explicit prior-versus-likelihood switch?
We fit the hierarchical Bayesian model to trial-level data from 12 participants who completed motion-direction estimation tasks with different conditions of sensory coherence and prior width. The model works by holding two priors simultaneously as a mixed distribution hyperprior. It also adds a hyperparameter that holds the confidence of the observer regarding the likelihood coming from a naive (uniform) prior or an informed (spiked von Mises) prior, which is updated over trials. We compare the model to a Switching observer model by penalized likelihood (AIC/BIC) and their ability to reproduce individual response distributions.
We hypothesize that a hierarchical Bayesian observer will account for the inter-subject variability in behavior. Specifically, we predict this Bayesian observer will (i) reproduce both unimodal and bimodal estimate distributions, and (ii) recover the block-specific prior widths that change through learning over multiple blocks, giving a principled account of how confidence in the prior evolves. These results would reframe the Switching observer’s discrete strategy as a graded Bayesian integration.
Keywords:
hierarchical Bayesian inference; perceptual estimation; prior confidence; online learning; motion direction
