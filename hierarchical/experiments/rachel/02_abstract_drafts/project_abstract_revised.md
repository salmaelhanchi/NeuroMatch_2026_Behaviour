## Abstract (revised)

Human perceptual estimates are often described by Bayesian models that integrate prior expectations with uncertain sensory evidence. However, in a motion-direction estimation task, participants produced response distributions with peaks near both the prior mean and the presented motion direction. This bimodality motivated a Switching observer that, on each trial, selects between a prior-based and a sensory-based estimate rather than combining them into a single posterior. Crucially, that account fits the prior as a fixed, already-learned quantity and treats the selection as a within-trial mechanism; how the prior itself is acquired was left unmodeled. The present project asks whether an explicit switch is still needed once the observer is allowed to learn its prior online. We implement a hierarchical Bayesian observer that updates its confidence in the prior trial by trial from the true motion direction revealed as feedback at the end of each trial. The prior is a mixed distribution — a peaked von Mises at the mean combined with a uniform component — and the strength of its peaked component is a latent quantity carried and updated across trials rather than fitted separately per block. We hypothesize that mixing over this evolving belief can reproduce both unimodal and bimodal response distributions and provide a competitive account of behavior relative to the Switching observer, with bimodality emerging from adaptive inference rather than an explicit selection rule.

The trial-level data from 12 participants who estimated the direction of briefly presented random-dot motion were analyzed. Sensory strength was manipulated using motion coherences of 6%, 12%, and 24%, while prior strength was manipulated across blocks using motion-direction distributions with standard deviations of 10°, 20°, 40°, and 80°. The principal variables are presented direction, reported direction, prior width, motion coherence, trial position within a block, and the model’s evolving estimate of prior confidence. The hierarchical Bayesian model will be fitted separately to each participant by maximizing the likelihood of their trial-level responses while preserving trial and block order. Model validity will be assessed through controlled simulations, parameter recovery, model recovery against the Switching observer, convergence of the learned prior confidence, and posterior-predictive checks. We will then implement the original Switching observer and compare the two models using negative log-likelihood, AIC, BIC, and sequence-preserving cross-validation, and by testing where bimodality arises: the Switching observer predicts two peaks within individual trials, whereas the learning account predicts bimodality that emerges only when trials are pooled across a block’s learning transient. This study will test whether switching-like perceptual behavior requires an explicit selection mechanism or can emerge from adaptive hierarchical Bayesian inference.

We expect the hierarchical model to reveal substantial individual differences in prior-confidence learning, explaining some subjects through continuous integration while approximating switching-like behavior in others. This work will clarify whether bimodal perceptual estimates require an explicit switching mechanism or can emerge from adaptive hierarchical inference, providing a more unified account of how humans learn and use uncertainty during perception.

Keywords: hierarchical Bayesian inference; online prior learning; perceptual estimation; prior confidence; switching observer
